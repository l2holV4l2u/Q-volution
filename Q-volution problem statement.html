Energy Grid Optimization

Track: Hardware | Difficulty: Advanced | Partner: Rigetti Computing

The Mission

Today's electrical grids require increasingly sophisticated management. Operators must balance the integration of renewable energy and the energy needs of AI infrastructure against the realities of fluctuating loads and weather events. These factors are pushing network optimization beyond the capabilities of traditional systems. To ensure stability and prevent service interruptions in this new era, we need to develop next-generation tools that can monitor the grid's health with unprecedented precision.
At the heart of this challenge lies a critical metric: the Maximum Power Energy Section (MPES). This quantity represents the partition of the grid capable of the highest aggregate power transfer, effectively revealing the theoretical limit of the network's capacity. Mathematically, calculating this metric requires solving a weighted maximum cut problem. Because finding the exact solution is famously intractable for classical computers, it has become the standard "Hello World!" example for the Quantum Approximate Optimization Algorithm (QAOA).
However, the size of the input problem exceeds simulator and quantum hardware capabilities for executing the standard QAOA. Your mission will be to come up with a strategy to tackle a problem that is too large to be naively handled through standard quantum optimization algorithms.

Technical Context

To translate the physical reality of the power grid into a computational model, we abstract the network into a weighted undirected graph G=(V,E)G = (V, E)G=(V,E). In this representation, the nodes correspond to the essential intersection points of the infrastructure — such as busbars, substations, and generators — where power is injected, routed, or consumed. The edges connecting these nodes represent the physical transmission lines and transformers that carry electricity across the network. The "weight" of each edge is derived from the physics of the connection, specifically the line's admittance. A higher weight signifies a connection with lower resistance to flow, representing a "stronger" electrical link.
To compute the MPES, one must partition the grid into two groups such that the power transferred from one group to the other — equal to the sum of the admittances on the lines — is maximized. Denoting the nodes in one group as +1+1+1 and the nodes in the other group as −1-1−1, this problem is equivalent to maximizing:

The Task

Your main goal is to maximize the objective function C(z⃗)C(\vec{z})C(z) using a quantum algorithm of your choice, aiming to find a solution as close as possible to the optimal MPES. While Problem A may be solvable with standard approaches, Problem B presents a significant challenge: the standard encoding of one qubit per node exceeds the capabilities of most current quantum emulators and hardware. Therefore, your task involves practical innovation.

1. Resource Estimation

Before attempting a solution, estimate what it would take to execute a standard QAOA circuit with ppp layers using the conventional one-node-per-qubit encoding. Detail the total qubit count, circuit depth, and/or the number of gates necessitated by the graph's connectivity, providing an explanation of why this brute-force mapping is infeasible on existing superconducting quantum computers with a square-grid topology.
Hints: Orders of magnitude and big-O notations are sufficient. Why can't large circuits be easily simulated on a classical computer? What limits executing large circuits on contemporary hardware? How can one practically achieve a two-qubit gate between qubits which aren't adjacent?

2. Optimization Challenge

Engineer a strategy to solve the input problems despite these limitations. Innovate beyond standard QAOA implementations — divide-and-conquer approaches, decomposition techniques, or other approximation methods. Explain your strategy, motivate it against other options, and be critical about its potential limitations.
Idea: Break down the input graph into smaller subgraphs handled independently, then reconstruct a final solution by concatenating subgraph solutions. NetworkX offers edge_betweenness_partition() for this.

3. Benchmarking

Validate your results by establishing the performance of your quantum solution against a chosen classical baseline. How do you make a fair benchmark of a quantum algorithm against a classical one? Discuss trade-offs between runtime and accuracy, analyze the bottlenecks of your proposed algorithm, and propose directions to overcome them.
Hint: NetworkX implements one_exchange(), a classical greedy heuristic for solving the weighted maximum cut problem.

4. Hardware Execution

Run, test, and debug on a simulator before targeting real hardware. Quantum hardware is noisy, has a specific topology, and only possesses specific gates. You can either co-design your algorithm around hardware constraints from the start, or compile at the end at the cost of additional gate overhead.
Hints: Rigetti's Ankaa-3 platform features the iSWAP two-qubit gate, and one-qubit RZ(θ) and RX(π/2) gates. Two-qubit gates are typically the main bottleneck due to lower fidelity. On hardware, target ~10-qubit circuits with ≤ 100 two-qubit gates unless using error mitigation and suppression techniques.
